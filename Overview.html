Web Accessibility Perspectives:
Explore the Impact and Benefits for Everyone

Introduction

Web accessibility is essential for people with disabilities but useful for many more. Learn about some of the accessibility considerations and how they benefit everyone in different situations.

Keyboard Compatibility

All functionality needs to work with the keyboard. That is, moving between controls using the tab key and other shortcuts, and typing into input fields. This includes moving between any links, buttons, forms, and other controls. Many websites support use with a mouse only. For example, they do not allow you to type in a date as well as to select it from a pop-up calendar.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with physical disabilities who cannot use the mouse;
    • People who are blind, and cannot see the mouse pointer on the screen;
    • People with chronical conditions, such as repetitive stress injuries (RSI), who should not use a mouse.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content works for people with temporary limitations, such as a broken arm or broken mouse;
    • Content works in more situations, such as with on-screen keyboards and terminals without a mouse.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Native HTML controls, like links, buttons, and form elements, work with the keyboard by default. Problems arise when custom-made controls, CSS styles, and scripts that control interaction break this compatibility. Also the navigation between multiple controls could cause barriers. For example, when the order of the controls in the source code does not match the visual presentation, or when there are too many controls without a way to jump between groups of related controls. Such issues occur particularly in forms, menus, and applications with many controls.

Colors with Good Contrast

There needs to be sufficent contrast between text and its background (technically called luminosity contrast ratio). This also includes text on images, icons, and buttons. Too little contrast makes the content difficult to read. Some people are also sensitive to bright colors.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with color blindess, who cannot discern certain colors;
    • People with color perception limitations and sensitivities.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content works in different lighting conditions, such as sunlight and glare, especially on mobile devices;
    • Content is easier to read by everyone, including those who do not have specific sight conditions.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    The selection of appropriate foreground and background colors is crucial. There are tools to help check and select appropriate color combinations. This is ideally done during the early design stage and the selection of color palettes.

  • Learn more
    • Accessibility Principle: Content is easier to see and hear
    • Getting Started: Provide sufficient contrast between foreground and background
    • Easy Check: Contrast ratio ("color contrast")

      • Mr. Lee, Online shopper with color blindness
      • Mr. Yunus, Retiree with low vision, hand tremor, and mild short-term memory loss

Clear Layout and Design

The different parts of a web page need to be easy to locate and identify. For example, sometimes it is not easy to identify navigation menus and their menu items. Also the labels for form fields and controls are difficult to identify and associate their correspondence.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with low vision who are using screen magnification and only seeing a portion of the screen at a time;
    • People with cognitive and learning disabilities who often need clarify and consistency to orient themselves on a website.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content is more usable for people who are new to the particular website or application;
    • Content is more usable for people who are not confident in using computers and the web;
    • Content is more usable for mobile device users who are seeing it on smaller screens, and possibly in a hurry or with distraction.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Emplyoing a functional design with appropriate structure and placement. For example, by grouping related input and controls on a form, and provding headers to identify such groups. This includes in the markup coding and visually. Also the consistency of presentation and behavior of web page components is important.

  • Learn more

      • Users can easily navigate, find content, and determine where they are

      • Ensure that interactive elements are easy to identify
      • Ensure that form elements include clearly associated labels
    • Easy Check: Forms, labels, and errors

Text to Speech

Content needs to be coded so that it can be read aloud by text to speech software. Many computers and mobile devices today have built in text to speech software. People with disabilities may use specialized text to speech software called screen readers. These can also highlight the text as it is being read aloud, for people to see and hear the content at the same time.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People who are blind and cannot see what is on the screen;
    • People who have partial sight (often legally blind) and cannot see certain types of content;
    • People with some forms of dyslexia and other cognitive and learning disabilities, who need to see and hear the text to better understand it.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content can be read aloud for people who cannot read the written language;
    • Content can be read aloud for people who prefer to listen, for example while multi-tasking.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Using semantic HTML markup for structures, such as headings, paragraphs, lists, forms, and tables is essential for text to speech to work. Also, text alternatives are necessary for images, icons, and other non-text content. Screen reading software announces these structures, and can also read text alternatives for images when they are present.

  • Learn more
    • Accessibility Principle:
      • Text alternatives for non-text content
      • Content can be presented in different ways
    • Getting Started:
      • Write meaningful text alternatives for images
      • Use headings to convey meaning and structure
      • Identify page language and language changes
    • Easy Check:
      • Image text alternatives ("alt text")
      • Basic Structure Check
    • User Story:
      • Ms. Laitinen, Accountant with blindness
      • Ms. Kaseem, Teenager with deaf-blindness
      • Ms. Olsen, Classroom student with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and dyslexia
    • WCAG 2.0: Success Criteria relating to "text to speech"
    • Mobile Web Design:
      • Non-text objects (images, sound, video) with no text alternative
      • Information conveyed only using CSS (visual formatting)
      • Non descriptive link label

Large Links, Buttons, and Controls

The area for clicking and tapping controls should be large enough for people to activate them. This includes links, buttons, checkboxes, and other controls. Small controls, or controls that are placed too closely to each other, are difficult to use by many people. This is particularly relevant on mobile devices with small screens.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with physical disabilities who have reduced dexterity;
    • People with chronical conditions, such as repetitive stress injuries (RSI), who need to avoid excessive movement.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content is more usable on touch screens, especially on smaller mobile devices;
    • Content is more usable for everyone not used to the mouse or touch-pad on the computer they are using.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Enlarging the size of the controls and activation areas around the controls brings improvement. Just increasing the font size of the control often isn't enough. Additionally, providing labels for controls, which is an accessibility requirement itself, often enlarges the activation area.

  • Learn more

    • Getting Started:
      • Create designs for different viewport sizes
      • Ensure that form elements include clearly associated labels
    • Easy Check: Forms, labels, and errors
    • User Story: Mr. Yunus, Retiree with low vision, hand tremor, and mild short-term memory loss
    • WCAG 2.0: Success Criteria relating to "labels"

Video Captions

All audio that is played together with visual content needs to be captioned. This includes video and animations. Captions are a text form of everything that is spoken in the audio (subtitles), and of important audio content. This includes information about who is speaking if it is not visible, any sound effects, music, laughter, and so on. Captions are synchronized with the visual content playing.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People who are deaf and cannot hear the audio;
    • People who are hard of hearing (often legally deaf) and cannot hear certain types of content;
    • People with some forms of dyslexia and other cognitive and learning disability, who need to see and hear the text to better understand it.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content can be used in loud environments where you cannot hear the audio;
    • Content can be used in silent environments where you cannot use audio;
    • Content can be better understood by people hearing and seeing the information, for example people who are less fluent in the language spoken.
    • Content can be more easily transcribed using the captions, which also improved indexing for search engines.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Captions need to be made available together with pre-recorded and live video content. Voice recognition software can be of great help for simple videos. Sometimes voice recognition software is built into the computer or the social media platform, such as YouTube. For many languages there are also captioning services that are quite affordable.

  • Learn more
    • Accessibility Principle: Captions and other alternatives for multimedia
    • Getting Started: Create transcripts and captions for multimedia
    • Easy Check: Multimedia (video, audio) alternatives
    • User Story: Ms. Martinez, Online student who is hard of hearing
    • WCAG 2.0: Success Criteria relating to "captions"
    • Mobile: Multimedia with no captions

Customizable Text

Users need to be able to change the size, spacing, font, and colors of the text. This includes that text re-flows properly when it is enlarged, and that no information or functionality is lost (text re-sizing is different from zooming or magnification, which enlarge the entire web page). Many web browsers provide customization options, though sometimes they are not easily findable. Some web browser plug-ins and mobile applications do not provide or adhere to such settings.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with low vision who are not using screen magnification software;
    • People with some forms of dyslexia and other cognitive and learning disabilities, who need particular presentation of the text to better read it.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content is more adaptable to smaller and also larger screen sizes;
    • Content is more adaptable to personal preferences and comfort;
    • Content is more ready for translation, to adapt to words and sentences of different lengths.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Content needs to be properly designed and coded, so that it can adapt to different customization options. This includes using relative rather than absolute units for the size of fonts, controls, and other objects. Applications should use the settings of the web browser or operating system. Websites and applications could also provide information to help users change their own settings.

  • Learn more

    • Getting Started: Write code that adapts to the user's technology
    • Easy Check: Resize text
    • User Story:
      • Mr. Yunus, Retiree with low vision, hand tremor, and mild short-term memory loss

Voice Recognition

Content needs to be properly designed and coded, so that it can be used through voice. This includes dictating text in a form field, as well as issuing commands to activate links, buttons, and other controls. Most computers and mobile devices today have built-in voice recognition software. Some voice recognition software allows complete control over computer interaction, including to scroll the screen, copy and paste text, and activate menus.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with physical disabilities who cannot use the keyboard or mouse;
    • People with chronical conditions, such as repetitive stress injuries (RSI), who should not use the keyboard or mouse.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content works for people with temporary limitations, such as a broken arm;
    • Content is more usable for people who prefer to speak rather than type, for example while multi-tasking.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Keyboard compatibility is the basis for voice recognition. In addition, labels and identifiers for controls in the source code need to match their visual presentation, so that it is clear which voice command will activate a control.

      • Provide alternative text for images

Understandable Content

Content needs to be easy to follow and understand by the majority of the target audience. In specific situations separate easy-to-read content may be necessary, for example for information relating to health and safety. For the majority of content this means simply avoiding overly complex sentences and jargon, and providing clear layout and design.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with learning disabilities who cannot understand complex sentence structures and vocabulary;
    • People with some forms of cognitive disabilities who have difficulty focusing on long passages of dense text.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content is more usable for people with lower language skills, such as non-native language users and people with low literacy;
    • Content is more easy to overview and skim, and to build a mental-model of the information.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Avoiding overly complex words, jargon, and acronyms, and providing explanations for these when they need to be used. Also providing structure using headings, lists, and spacing is important. Illustrations are often helpful. Indicating orientation cues, such as during a purchasing process or in a series of instructions, also helps. Much of these presentation aspects correspond with the clear layout and design feature.

      • Provide clear instructions
      • Use headings to convey meaning and structure

Notifications and Feedback

Users need to be kept informed about what is going on, and to receive appropriate feedback during interaction. This includes confirmation messages when actions are completed, such as when forms are submitted. Also error messages should help rather than confuse users.

  • Who depends on this feature?
    • People with some forms of cognitive and learning disabilities, who may be easily distracted by unexpected behavior and confusing error messages.
  • What are the additional benefits?
    • Content is more usable for people who are new to the particular website or application;
    • Content is more usable for people who are not confident in using computers and the web.
  • What needs to happen for this to work?

    Use clear and simple language to make error messages more understandable. For example, describe how to fix an error. If a form has been submitted, make sure to follow it with a confirmation message. If a change is being made on the screen, such as the appearance of a loading spinner, or new content being injected, make sure that users are alerted to this change.
